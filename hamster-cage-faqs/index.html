Hamster cage questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best hamster cage gives your pet enough floor space, good ventilation, and a safe layout. Aim for a single-level area of at least several hundred square inches, wire or bin cages are popular choices, and avoid habitats with dangerously wide bar spacing or too many tubes.

What size cage does a hamster need?

Size is the most important factor in a hamster’s well-being. Syrian hamsters, the largest common pet species, need more space than dwarfs. As a rule of thumb, a continuous floor area of at least several hundred square inches is recommended by many owners and experts, with larger being better. Avoid tall, multi-tiered setups with small platforms that don’t add usable floor space. Hamsters are ground-dwelling animals and benefit from a long, unbroken run than a climb. A single-level cage with a deep base (for bedding) is ideal. When comparing cages, ignore total volume or tube length, focus on the uninterrupted floor area. Many popular wire cages sold as “starter kits” are actually too small for full-time housing, so measure before buying.

What type of cage is best: wire, aquarium, or bin?

Each type has trade-offs. Wire cages (with a solid plastic base) offer the best ventilation and allow for hanging accessories, but the bars must be spaced closely enough that even a dwarf hamster cannot squeeze through. Generally, horizontal bars are preferred over vertical ones because they enable climbing. Glass aquariums or tanks provide a draft-free environment and deep bedding without mess, but they are heavy and can become stuffy without a mesh lid. They work well for dwarf species that might slip through standard bar spacing. Large plastic storage bins modified with a wire mesh top are a budget-friendly, DIY alternative. They offer deep space for burrowing and are easy to clean, but ventilation holes need to be ample and secure. Many experienced keepers recommend bin cages for their low cost and high practicality.

What about bar spacing and material?

Bar spacing is critical for safety. For Syrian hamsters, spacing should be no more than about half an inch to prevent escape or injury. For dwarf hamsters, the gap must be even smaller, around a quarter of an inch or less. Measure the gap between bars rather than guessing by eye. Metal bars should be coated with a non-toxic, chew-safe finish. Avoid cages with plastic bases that are too shallow, as hamsters need at least a few inches of bedding for burrowing. Also check that the cage door openings are large enough for you to reach in comfortably and clean or interact with your pet.

What bedding should I use?

Aspen shavings, paper-based bedding, or hemp bedding are the safest choices. They absorb moisture well and allow hamsters to tunnel. Avoid cedar or pine shavings because the aromatic oils can cause respiratory problems. Depth matters: provide at least a few inches of bedding across the entire cage floor, with a deeper area (six inches or more in one corner) for burrowing. Some owners use a mix, paper bedding for softness and aspen for odor control. Do not use fluffy, cotton-like “nesting” materials sold for small animals; they can entangle paws or be ingested and cause blockages. Unbleached toilet paper or hay (from a pet store) makes a safe alternative for nest building.

What accessories are essential for a hamster cage?

A solid-surface exercise wheel is non-negotiable. For Syrians the wheel should be at least eight inches in diameter, for dwarfs at least six to seven inches, to prevent back arching. Avoid wire or mesh wheels that can catch tiny feet. Hamsters also need a water bottle with a sipper tube and a heavy ceramic food bowl that can’t be tipped over. Provide at least one enclosed hideout (wood, ceramic, or plastic) where the hamster can sleep undisturbed. Chew toys made from untreated wood, woven grass balls, and cardboard tubes help wear down ever-growing teeth. A sand bath (using reptile sand or chinchilla dust-free sand) is appreciated by many hamsters for grooming and is healthier than water baths. Avoid dust products meant for chinchillas as they can irritate hamster lungs.

Where should I place the hamster cage?

Choose a quiet, low-traffic area away from direct sunlight, drafts, heating vents, or air conditioners. Hamsters are sensitive to temperature swings and loud noises. A stable room temperature around 68 to 75 degrees Fahrenheit is ideal. Keep the cage off the floor to help the hamster feel safer and to avoid drafts. A sturdy table or dedicated stand works well. Avoid placing the cage in a bedroom if you are a light sleeper, as hamsters are nocturnal and will be active at night with wheel and chewing noises. Also consider visibility: you’ll want to easily spot any health issues (e.g., lack of movement, soiled fur) and enjoy watching your pet, but the cage should have a calm corner for daytime sleep.

Frequently asked questions

Can I keep two hamsters in the same cage?

Most hamsters are solitary and territorial. Syrian hamsters must live alone after about eight weeks of age. Some dwarf species like Roborovskis can sometimes cohabitate if introduced young and given a very large cage, but conflicts are common. For beginners and most owners, one per cage is the safest rule.

How often should I clean the hamster cage?

Spot-clean wet or soiled bedding daily or every other day. A deep clean of the entire cage (replace all bedding and wash accessories) should happen about once every one to four weeks, depending on the size of the cage and the number of hamsters. Leave a small amount of old bedding after cleaning to keep their familiar scent and reduce stress.

Are tube-and-tower cages a good choice?

They are often marketed as fun but can have poor ventilation and are difficult to clean thoroughly. Tubes can trap urine and become unsanitary. Hamsters may also get stuck in narrow tubing. A simpler, open cage with solid floors is usually healthier and easier to maintain.

What should I do if my hamster chews the plastic cage base?

Chewing is normal, but excessive chewing on plastic can lead to ingestion of sharp pieces. Try providing more chew toys and wooden gnaw items. If the chewing persists, consider switching to a cage with a glass or metal base, or a bin cage with high, chew-proof walls.

Can I use a fish tank for a hamster?

Yes, an aquarium with a secure, well-ventilated mesh lid can make an excellent hamster home, especially for burrowing. Ensure the tank is long rather than tall, at least a 40-gallon breeder size for a Syrian. Keep in mind that aquariums are heavy, and good airflow must be maintained.

Do hamsters need a wheel?

Absolutely. A suitable, solid-surface wheel provides crucial exercise and mental stimulation. Without a wheel, hamsters may become stressed, overweight, or develop health problems. Choose a properly sized wheel that allows a flat back during running.

In shortChoosing the right hamster cage comes down to floor space, safe materials, and easy maintenance. Focus on giving your hamster room to burrow and run, keep bar spacing tight, and skip overcomplicated tube setups. With a solid foundation cage plus the right bedding and wheel, you’ll set up a happy, healthy home for your pet.
